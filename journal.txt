Sobre la implementación

Owen (1998) plantea las ecuaciones 10 y 11 (pag 79)
y se las atribuye a McKay. Esto no es cierto pero lo
he leído en muchos lados. La primera forma de esas
ecuaciones hasta donde yo puedo ver salen de Iman,
Davenport, Zeigler (1980) pag. 5. y aún en ese caso
no es la forma que describe Owen.
La fórmula de Owen sale por primera vez hasta donde
he podido ver en Stein (1982). Hay dos diferencias.
La primera es que en Stein fórmula 2, pag 144 X_jk
ya toma en cuenta la distribución inversa y además
usa "N^-1". Ambos cambios son estéticos porque
Davenport multipica por 0.125 = 1/8 y Davenport hace
F^-1 en la tabla II de la página 5.
El otro cambio es que Stein usa p_jk que es el
vector ya permutado mientras que Davenport lo usa
en orden. Dado que Davenport habla sobre permutar los
vectores antes creo que sí los permuta pero no me
consta porque el código incluido en el artículo
es un infierno para poder leerlo entonces los ignoro.
Voy a suponer que efectivamente Stein y Davenport
hacen lo mismo.

https://github.com/ziglang/zig/issues/8708

The Linux kernel ignores the requested stack size from
the ELF file, and always gives an 8 MiB stack. Compensate
for this by, at runtime, using the setrlimit syscall
to increase the stack size to match the requested stack size.

Here's an example of doing it with a hard coded value of 16 MiB:

    if (@import("builtin").target.os.tag == .linux) {
        std.os.setrlimit(.STACK, .{
            .cur = 16 * 1024 * 1024,
            .max = 16 * 1024 * 1024,
        }) catch {
            @panic("unable to increase stack size");
        };
    }

This issue is to additionally use the auxiliary vector
in the start code to observe the GNU_STACK program
header, read the memsz, and use that for setrlimit.
This will honor the value set by the linker.

What Akizuki proves

In “Teilerkettensatz und Vielfachenkettensatz”, Yasuo
Akizuki proves (in modern language) that if a module
satisfies the descending chain condition (Artinian),
then it also satisfies the ascending chain condition
(Noetherian), under suitable finiteness control via
annihilators. This is an early form of what later
became known as the Hopkins–Levitzki theorem.

Setup

Let M be a module over a ring R.

Assume that every descending chain of submodules
stabilizes (so M is Artinian).

Akizuki’s method relies on:

a minimal counterexample argument
the use of annihilators
reduction to cyclic extensions
chains of ideals (called “Teilerketten”)

Step 1: Minimal counterexample

Assume the statement is false.

Then there exists an Artinian module M that is
not Noetherian.

Choose such an M minimal with respect to the
descending chain condition (this is possible
because descending chains terminate).

Thus:

M is Artinian
M is not Noetherian
every proper factor module of M is Noetherian

Step 2: Maximal non-finitely generated submodule

Since M is not Noetherian, there exists a submodule
that is not finitely generated.

Choose a submodule N of M that is maximal with
respect to not being finitely generated.

Then:

N is not finitely generated
for any element x not in N, the module N + Rx is
finitely generated

Step 3: Reduction to a cyclic quotient

Pick x not in N.

Then M/N is generated by the image of x, so it is cyclic.

Since M is Artinian, M/N is also Artinian.

Now consider the annihilator of x modulo N:
Ann(x̄) = { r in R such that r x lies in N }

This ideal plays a central role.

Step 4: Chain of annihilators

Akizuki constructs an ascending chain of annihilators:
Ann(x) ⊆ Ann(x1) ⊆ Ann(x2) ⊆ ...

These arise from refining elements needed to
approximate generators of N.

Because M is Artinian, such chains must stabilize.

This is the key “Teilerkettensatz” idea: chains of
annihilators cannot grow indefinitely.

Step 5: Consequences of stabilization

From the stabilization of annihilators, Akizuki
deduces that only finitely many steps are needed
to build generators.

In other words:

one cannot construct an infinite strictly
increasing chain of submodules attempts to
enlarge generating sets eventually stop
producing new information

Thus N must actually be finitely generated.

Step 6: Contradiction

But N was chosen to be not finitely generated.

This is a contradiction.

Conclusion

Therefore, the assumption was false. Every
Artinian module in this setting must be Noetherian.

Remarks

Akizuki’s proof differs from modern presentations
in several ways:

it emphasizes annihilator chains rather than module length
it uses minimal counterexamples instead of composition series
it works in an ideal-theoretic style rather than fully
module-theoretic language

This is why the argument feels more technical
and less streamlined than later proofs, but it
is historically the first of its kind.

======================================================================
 FIRST-TWO-YEAR CHEMISTRY BOOKS - MASTER LIST WITH PREREQUISITES
 (deduplicated across the QS 2026 top-15 universities)
======================================================================

 "A -> B" means: have A's material before B makes full sense.
 Books in the same tier are ALTERNATIVES (pick one), not a
 sequence, unless an explicit arrow connects them.


----------------------------------------------------------------------
 PREREQUISITE MAP
----------------------------------------------------------------------

     +-------------------------------------+
     |    GENERAL / FOUNDATION CHEMISTRY    |
     |    (prerequisite for everything)     |
     +-------------------------------------+
                        |
        +---------------+---------------+
        |               |               |
        v               v               v
  +-----------+   +-----------+   +-----------+
  |  ORGANIC  |   | PHYSICAL  |   | INORGANIC |
  +-----------+   +-----------+   +-----------+
        |               |
        v               v
  +-----------+   +-----------+
  |  SYKES    |   |  ATKINS'  |
  | mechanism |   |Phys. Chem.|
  +-----------+   +-----------+

 The ONLY hard "read-first" pairs:
   1. General chemistry -> organic / physical / inorganic
   2. Elements of Physical Chemistry -> Atkins' Physical Chemistry
   3. Any organic text -> Sykes' mechanism guidebook


----------------------------------------------------------------------
 TIER 0  -  CONCEPTUAL PRIMERS (optional on-ramp)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
   - Keeler & Wothers - Why Chemical Reactions Happen
        -> primer for organic + physical
   - Keeler & Wothers - Chemical Structure and Reactivity
        -> bridges foundation into organic + inorganic (Cambridge)


----------------------------------------------------------------------
 TIER 1  -  GENERAL / FOUNDATION CHEMISTRY
           prerequisite for ALL below; alternatives, pick one
----------------------------------------------------------------------
   - Oxtoby, Gillis & Butler - Principles of Modern Chemistry
   - Atkins & Jones - Chemical Principles: The Quest for Insight
   - Brown, LeMay et al. - Chemistry: The Central Science
   - Burrows et al. - Chemistry3
   - Housecroft & Constable - Chemistry   (all-in-one, ETH)
   - Mortimer - Chemie                    (German, ETH)
   - Hua Tongwen - Principles of General Chemistry   (Peking)
   - Gao Song & Wang Yingxia - University Chemistry  (Peking)


----------------------------------------------------------------------
 TIER 2  -  BRANCH TEXTS
           each assumes Tier 1; the branches are independent
----------------------------------------------------------------------

 ORGANIC  (assumes bonding, structure, equilibrium)
   alternatives:
   - Clayden, Greeves, Warren & Wothers - Organic Chemistry
   - Vollhardt & Schore - Organic Chemistry: Structure and
       Function
   - Loudon & Parise - Organic Chemistry
   - McMurry - Organic Chemistry
   - Xing Qiyi et al. - Fundamental Organic Chemistry  (Peking)
        any of the above -> Sykes (Tier 3)

 PHYSICAL  (assumes Tier 1 + introductory calculus)
   - Atkins & de Paula - Elements of Physical Chemistry
        -> Atkins & de Paula - Atkins' Physical Chemistry (full)
   - McQuarrie & Simon - Physical Chemistry: A Molecular
       Approach   (standalone alternative to the Atkins line)
   - Fu Xiancai et al. - Physical Chemistry  (Peking/Nanjing)

 INORGANIC  (assumes atomic structure, bonding)
   alternatives:
   - Weller, Overton, Rourke & Armstrong - Inorganic Chemistry
        (the Shriver & Atkins successor)
   - Housecroft & Sharpe - Inorganic Chemistry
   - Riedel / Janiak - Anorganische Chemie   (German, ETH)
   - Wuhan / Jilin Univ. - Inorganic Chemistry   (Peking)


----------------------------------------------------------------------
 TIER 3  -  WITHIN-BRANCH DEEPENER
----------------------------------------------------------------------
   - Sykes - A Guidebook to Mechanism in Organic Chemistry
        requires: having started an organic text (Tier 2)

======================================================================
